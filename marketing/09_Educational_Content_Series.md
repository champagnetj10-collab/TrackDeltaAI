## Topic 1: Reading Telemetry

**Core teaching points:**
- Speed trace: where you're fast, where you're slow, and — critically — the *shape* of the speed curve through a corner (a rounded minimum = good line/rotation, a sharp V = likely understeer or a late apex)
- Brake trace: initial bite, peak pressure, release curve. A smooth taper is different from a hard stab.
- Throttle trace: on/off (binary) vs. modulated application — the shape tells you as much as the timing
- Steering trace: smoothness and correction frequency — lots of small corrections mid-corner usually signals the car (or the driver) is unsettled
- How all four traces line up against the track map / LapDistPct — reading them in isolation is much less useful than reading them together at the same corner

**Language guidance:** this is the one topic where defining basic terms is appropriate even for a competitive audience — many iRacing drivers have opened the built-in telemetry tool and never been taught how to actually read it.

**Short-form:** SF-37 ("Reading a brake trace in 20 seconds")
**Long-form:** LF-14 ("The Complete Beginner's Guide to Reading Telemetry")

## Topic 2: Braking

**Core teaching points:**
- Brake point ≠ skill by itself — it's brake point *combined with* modulation and consistency that matters
- Trail braking: carrying light pressure past turn-in, blending into the apex, to help rotate the car — not universal, some corners/cars want a fully released brake before turn-in
- Brake modulation quality: smooth taper vs. abrupt release — abrupt releases upset weight transfer and often cost mid-corner speed
- Consistency of brake point across laps matters more for race pace than a single heroic late-braking lap
- Common failure mode: braking later without adjusting anything else, which just moves the mistake later rather than removing it

**Short-form:** SF-31 (late braking), SF-32 (trail braking 20s explainer)
**Long-form:** LF-03 (brake point feel vs. reality), LF-15 (trail braking complete breakdown)

## Topic 3: Throttle

**Core teaching points:**
- Corner exit usually contains more available lap time than corner entry, especially on tracks with long straights following slow corners
- Throttle application point relative to the apex — too early costs mid-corner rotation/stability, too late costs exit speed
- Modulation vs. binary application — progressive throttle roll-on almost always beats an on/off stab, especially in lower-grip conditions
- Full-throttle fraction as a session-level signal of overall aggression/confidence, not just a per-corner stat

**Short-form:** SF-33 (throttle application basics)
**Long-form:** LF-04 (the throttle mistake costing a second a lap)

## Topic 4: Racing Lines

**Core teaching points:**
- The "ideal line" taught in most guides is a geometric starting point (late apex, use full track width) — not a universal answer for every car/driver/condition
- Personal factors that shift the truly optimal line: your own braking style, the car's specific balance, tire wear state, and even what corner comes *next* (sacrificing one corner's speed for a better exit into a long straight)
- Multi-corner sequences (esses, chicanes) require thinking one or two corners ahead, not optimizing each corner in isolation
- Track-specific nuance: call out real examples (e.g., a specific named corner at a seeded track) rather than staying abstract

**Short-form:** SF-34 (racing line isn't universal)
**Long-form:** LF-06 (the line everyone gets wrong at a specific track)

## Topic 5: Racecraft

**Core teaching points:**
- Racecraft is a bundle of distinct skills, not one thing: clean overtaking, defending a position within the rules, tire/fuel management across a stint, and reading the race unfolding around you
- Why a driver can be fast in practice/quali and inconsistent in the race — quali is one hot lap under low pressure with no traffic; a race is 20+ consistent laps under pressure, in traffic, managing a resource (tires/fuel)
- Incident rate and risk profile as measurable racecraft-adjacent signals, even though full wheel-to-wheel racecraft coaching requires more than telemetry (video review, race context) — be honest about this boundary rather than overclaiming
- Pressure performance: the gap between early-session and late-session pace, and between qualifying and race pace, as a trainable, measurable thing

**Short-form:** SF-35 (racecraft definition), SF-36 (quali vs race pace), SF-40 (track limits/incident rate)
**Long-form:** LF-07 (iRating vs actually being fast), LF-11 (qualifying vs race pace), LF-17 (racecraft: skills lap time doesn't measure)

## Topic 6: Consistency (cross-cutting — touches all four skills above)

**Core teaching points:**
- Consistency is measurable (lap time standard deviation / coefficient of variation, hot-lap percentage), not a feeling
- A driver who is 0.3s slower on their best lap but far more consistent lap-to-lap will usually out-race a spikier, faster-on-paper driver over a full stint
- Consistency is trainable through deliberate, single-focus practice (this is directly why TrackDelta's "one clear objective" per debrief exists — focus produces consistency faster than trying to fix five things at once)

**Short-form:** SF-12 (brake point consistency visualized), SF-38 (consistency beats one fast lap)
**Long-form:** LF-16 (consistency is a skill you can train)

## Content Format Variety for Education Topics

Don't let every educational piece be "founder talks to camera." Rotate through:
- **Screen-recording breakdown** — narrated telemetry trace or debrief walkthrough
- **Whiteboard/animated diagram** — good for racing line and multi-corner sequence topics, which benefit from a top-down track view
- **Talking head + on-screen text callouts** — good for definition-style short-form ("Trail braking in 20 seconds")
- **Real footage + overlay** — onboard replay footage from an actual (or illustrative) session with the relevant telemetry trace overlaid at the bottom of frame
